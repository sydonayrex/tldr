# Anchor Playbook Integration

## Source
"The Anchor Playbook" — a guide to engineering leadership on balanced teams. Describes the "anchor" role (engineering practice representative) and 8 "anchortypes" observed across projects.

## What Was Integrated

### Into Architecture Process Skill
- The Anchor role definition (IS NOT tech lead/boss/manager — IS facilitator/teacher/advocate)
- 8 anchortypes: Peer, Servant Leader, Shield, Constant, Expert, Learner, Captain, Bridge Builder
- Each with responsibilities, challenges, and considerations
- 5 anchor scenarios: Introducing, Program Onboarding, Rotation, Cross-Team Neural Network, Learning Anchorship

### Into Architecture Process Skill (ADRs)
- Architectural Decision Records: why, what, future structure
- Storage: in repo, docs/adr/, Markdown, YYYY-MM-DD naming
- Benefits: review past thoughts, learn from mistakes, self-documenting for future team members

## Key Concepts

### Anchor vs Traditional Leadership
The anchor role exists because empowered balanced teams are incompatible with hierarchical leadership. Anchors facilitate rather than decide, teach rather than direct, and advocate rather than manage.

### When to Use Each Anchortype
- **Peer**: Strong, mature team that needs light-touch leadership
- **Servant Leader**: Team that needs facilitation and coaching
- **Shield**: Team that needs protection from interruptions and blockers
- **Constant**: Long-running project needing context preservation
- **Expert**: Client-facing project needing technical credibility
- **Learner**: Low-risk project where a new anchor can develop
- **Captain**: Last resort for teams that cannot move forward
- **Bridge Builder**: Multi-team programs needing cross-team coordination

## Pitfall: Source Anonymization
The original playbook referenced "Pivots" (Pivotal Labs employees), "Labs," and specific organizational terms. All were stripped during integration. The anchor concept is generic — it describes a role, not a job title.
