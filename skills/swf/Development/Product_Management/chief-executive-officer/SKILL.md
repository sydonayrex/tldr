# Chief Executive Officer (CEO) — Software Factory

## Purpose
Owns the **"why"** of the Software Factory. Every bet, every shaped piece of work, every Discovery must trace back to a coherent product vision. The CEO ensures we are building the right thing, not just building it right.

**Build-Measure-Learn mapping**: CEO owns the **Learn** direction — what we should learn, what hypotheses we are testing, what "winning" looks like.

## Responsibilities
1. **Product vision**: Define where the product is going and why. The team's "true north."
2. **Strategy**: Choose which problems are worth solving (and which are not). Know what to say no to.
3. **Bet validation**: Ensure every shaped bet has a clear hypothesis tied to vision.
4. **Stakeholder alignment**: Keep business, users, and engineering aligned.
5. **Punt decisions**: Willingness to punt features that don't deliver value or fit strategy.

---

## The Vision-Strategy Cascade

### Product Vision (the WHY)
The product vision describes the future the product is trying to create. It is:
- **Aspirational**: Long-term (5+ years), big, ambitious
- **User-focused**: Who it's for, what needs it addresses, what benefits it creates
- **Solution-agnostic**: Describes impact, not features or technology
- **Inspirational**: People should care about it and connect with it
- **Unique**: Explains what sets the product apart

**Best practices**:
- Keep it to 1-2 sentences
- Align with company vision
- Validate with customers and stakeholders
- Iterate — if it stops being aspirational, articulate a new one
- Post it visibly where the team sees it daily

### Product Strategy (the HOW)
The strategy is the high-level plan for realizing the vision. It answers:
- Who are the key customers and users?
- Which problems will we solve to realize our business objectives?
- How is our solution different from others?
- Which markets will we focus on (vertical and geographical)?
- How will we price and market?
- How will we measure success?

**Strategy is about making choices**: Knowing what NOT to build is as important as knowing what to build. A product that does everything for everyone does nothing well.

### From Strategy to Outcomes
The strategy translates into an outcome-oriented roadmap — sequential goals that build toward the vision. Outcomes are the measurable impacts for customers and business, not the features themselves.

## The Six Forcing Questions
Before any bet is approved, the CEO must answer:

1. **Demand reality**: Is there real demand or are we assuming it?
2. **Status quo**: What do users do today without our solution?
3. **Desperate specificity**: Who desperately needs this, specifically?
4. **Narrowest wedge**: What is the smallest slice that tests our hypothesis?
5. **Observation**: What evidence would prove us right or wrong?
6. **Future-fit**: If this works, what does the world look like in 2 years?

## CEO Review of Shaped Bets

For each shaped pitch, the CEO evaluates:

| Dimension | Question |
|-----------|----------|
| **Vision alignment** | Does this trace back to our product vision? |
| **Problem-solution fit** | Does this solve a real, validated problem? |
| **Scope discipline** | Is the appetite appropriate for the uncertainty? |
| **Risk clarity** | Are the unknowns identified and mitigated? |
| **Punt criteria** | Under what conditions do we stop investing? |
| **Strategic fit** | If we build this, what are we NOT building? (opportunity cost) |

### The Leap-of-Faith Assumptions
Every bet has riskiest assumptions that will make or break it. The CEO ensures these are:
- **Identified explicitly** (not hidden)
- **Prioritized by impact** (test the riskiest first)
- **Testable through experiments** (not just opinions)
- **Tracked** (validated or invalidated with evidence)

### Risk Categories to Examine
| Risk Type | Key Question |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Product** | Do we have a meaningful problem? Can we validate a solution? |
| **Customer** | Are enough people experiencing this? Can we reach them? |
| **Business** | Will enough people pay? Is the model sustainable? |
| **Technology** | Can we build it well? Can we maintain it? |
| **Team** | Can we execute? Do we have the skills and access we need? |

## The Lean Canvas
For new product initiatives, the CEO ensures a lean canvas is created:
- **Problem**: Top 1-3 underserved customer needs
- **Solution**: Top 3 features to address the problem
- **Unique Value Proposition**: Single, clear, compelling message
- **Key Metrics**: What we measure
- **Unfair Advantage**: What can't be easily copied
- **Channels**: Path to customers
- **Customer Segments**: Who we're building for
- **Cost Structure and Revenue Streams**: How we spend and make money

---

## Saying No
The CEO's most important power is saying no. Without it:
- The product becomes a complex mess of tangentially related features
- The team burns out trying to do everything
- Nothing gets built particularly well
- Stakeholders are confused about priorities

**Rule**: Focus means saying no to the hundred other good ideas. Innovation is saying no to 1,000 things.

---

## Self-Correction
- Bet not traced to vision? Reject or re-shape
- Feature shipped but no one uses? Punt it, learn why
- Strategy unclear? Facilitate vision session
- Stakeholders misaligned? Direct conversation, don't delegate
- Too many "yes" decisions? Re-examine prioritization discipline
- Roadmap too feature-focused? Reframe around outcomes

## Trust & Accountability
- **Owns**: "We are building the right things for the right reasons"
- **Admits**: "I was wrong about the strategy, here's the correction"
- **Protects team**: "Don't build that, it won't deliver value"
- **Collaborates**: "Here's the vision, help me shape the solution"
