# Product Management

## Purpose
Owns the product from vision through delivery. Ensures the team builds the right thing (desirable), that helps the business (viable), and can be built well (feasible). Balances these three perspectives through continuous de-risking, outcome-oriented planning, and tight feedback loops.

**Build-Measure-Learn mapping**: Product Management owns the **integration point** of Build-Measure-Learn — translating learning into strategy, strategy into outcomes, outcomes into backlog items, and backlog items into validated learning.

## The Product Manager's Core Responsibilities

| # | Responsibility | Purpose |
|---|---------------|---------|
| 1 | Articulate product vision and strategy | So the team knows why they're building and what the plan is |
| 2 | Create an outcome-oriented roadmap | So priorities are clear and stakeholders understand direction |
| 3 | Establish measurable objectives | So we know if we're on the right path |
| 4 | Continually de-risk product direction | So we only build features we should build |
| 5 | Prioritize features | So we're always working on the most valuable thing |
| 6 | Manage the backlog | So the team has shared focus across tactical work |
| 7 | Decide when to ship | So we deliver value and validate learning frequently |
| 8 | Help maintain sustainable pace | So the team can go fast forever without burning out |
| 9 | Communicate effectively | So team, stakeholders, and sponsors stay aligned |

## The Vision-Strategy-Roadmap Cascade

### Product Vision (the WHY)
- Describes the future we're trying to create
- Highly aspirational, long-term (5+ years)
- The team's "true north" — guides every decision
- Answers: Who are we building for? What needs do we address? What benefits do we create?

**Best practices for a compelling vision**:
- Keep it short (1-2 sentences)
- Align with company vision
- Focus on the user
- Avoid specific solutions (stay flexible)
- Make it big and ambitious
- Be inspirational — people should care
- Be unique — what sets us apart
- Iterate as you learn
- Validate with customers and stakeholders

### Product Strategy (the HOW)
- A high-level plan for realizing the vision
- Spans several years
- Answers: Who are key customers? Which problems do we solve? How are we different? Which markets? How do we price and market? How do we measure success?

**Strategy is about making choices**: Knowing what NOT to build is as important as knowing what to build. A product that does everything for everyone does nothing well for anyone.

### Product Roadmap (the WHAT NEXT)
- Describes how the product will evolve over time
- Sequential goals that build towards the vision
- Organized across three time horizons:

| Horizon | Description | Clarity |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Current** | Work being done now | Crystal clear |
| **Near term** | Work coming up soon | Clear outcomes, features TBD |
| **Future** | Work to research more | Directional, likely to change |

**Roadmap design principles**:
1. Communicate direction to realize the vision
2. Assume uncertainty; optimize for learning and responding
3. Frame work around desired outcomes, not outputs
4. Easy to update and share

**Outcomes matter more than features**: The roadmap emphasizes results (improved metrics) rather than features. This creates agreement on what success looks like while enabling flexibility in solution.

## Outcome-Oriented Planning

### Plan Backwards from Vision and Strategy
1. Identify key challenges to overcome
2. Define desired outcomes (measurable impacts for customers and business)
3. Capture assumptions about how to reach those outcomes
4. Prioritize assumptions to validate

### Ask Before Prioritizing Work
- What dependencies need to be solved first?
- What do we need to learn first?
- What are the learning objectives that will make an impact?

### Goals, Objectives, and KPIs
| Concept | Timeframe | Nature | Example |
|---------|-----------|--------|---------|
| **Goal** | Long | Strategic, may not be directly measurable | "Improve customer satisfaction" |
| **Objective** | Medium | SMART: specific, measurable, attainable, realistic, time-bound | "Improve site performance" |
| **KPI** | Ongoing | Metric tied to objective | "Increase site availability from 97.5% to 99.5%" |

### Beware of Vanity Metrics
Good metrics are:
- **Actionable**: Tie back to specific, repeatable tasks
- **Comparative**: Ratios, not totals (percentage of users who purchase, not total users)
- **Time-enabled**: Show change over time

Vanity metrics (total signups, total downloads, cumulative anything) only go up and offer no insight into what to do next.

## Continually De-Risk Product Direction

### The Five Risk Categories
| Risk Type | Key Questions |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Product** | Do we have a meaningful problem? Can we validate a useful solution? Can we scale it? |
| **Customer** | Are enough people experiencing this pain? Can we reach them? At scale? |
| **Business** | Will enough people pay enough? Is the business model sustainable? |
| **Technology** | Can we implement well? Can we maintain and evolve it? |
| **Team** | Can we move effectively? Do we have access to users? Can we ship? Can we sustain pace? |

### De-Risking Process
1. Identify the riskiest assumptions
2. Prioritize by potential impact
3. Design experiments to test them
4. Run experiments and analyze data
5. Determine: validated or invalidated
6. Adjust plans based on learnings

### The Lean Canvas
A one-page tool for documenting and validating the riskiest business model hypotheses:
- **Problem**: Top 1-3 underserved customer needs
- **Solution**: Top 3 features to address the problem
- **Unique Value Proposition**: Single, clear, compelling message
- **Key Metrics**: Key activities we measure
- **Unfair Advantage**: Something that can't be easily bought or copied
- **Channels**: Path to customers
- **Customer Segments**: Target customers and users
- **Cost Structure**: Fixed and variable costs
- **Revenue Streams**: Sources of revenue

## Lean Experimentation

### Hypothesis-Driven Development
Treat product development as a series of experiments. Each experiment has three parts:

| Part | Template |
|------|----------|
| **Hypothesis** | We believe that <this capability> will result in <this outcome>. |
| **Test** | We'll do/make <this test>. |
| **Validation Criteria** | We'll know our hypothesis is valid if we observe/measure <this outcome>. |

**Rule**: Test only one variable per hypothesis. Otherwise you can't get reliable data.

### Experiment Techniques by Stage

| Stage | Assumptions | Experiment Type |
|-------|------------|-----------------|
| Problem/Solution Fit | Problem definition, Customer segments | Customer interviews |
| Problem/Solution Fit | Solution definition, Validation | Low-fidelity prototypes |
| Product/Market Fit | MVP definition | Concierge test, Wizard of Oz, Smoke test |
| Scale | Business model | A/B tests |

### Experiment Type Details
| Type | What It Does | When to Use |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Customer interviews** | Understand problems, how customers want to solve them, who early adopters are | Problem discovery |
| **Concierge test** | Replaces automated solution with humans interacting directly with customers | Validate whether anyone wants the solution before building |
| **Wizard of Oz test** | Replaces backend with humans so customer thinks they're using automation | Validate product desire without building backend |
| **Low-fidelity prototypes** | Rough, early, testable solution concepts (paper sketches OK) | Validate solution concepts early |
| **High-fidelity prototypes** | Clickable/tappable detailed representations | Validate concepts midway/late in design |
| **Walking skeleton** | Implements tiny end-to-end function | Validate technical feasibility |
| **Smoke test** | Landing page describing value proposition with signup | Validate whether anyone wants the product enough to act |
| **A/B test** | Two versions to compare performance | Optimize with large user sets |

### MVP is a Learning Milestone
The MVP is the smallest version of the product that enables maximum validated learning with the least effort. It is NOT:
- The first version you feel comfortable showing executives
- A proof of concept
- A minimum set of features without business goals
- Always software

The MVP is often not more than a few features that together help the user do something valuable. Deliver thin slices of the full value proposition, not one component at a time.

## Feature Prioritization

### The Prioritization Mindset
"People think focus means saying yes to the thing you've got to focus on. But that's not what it means at all. It means saying no to the hundred other good ideas that there are."

### Minimum Criteria for Building a Feature
Every feature should:
1. Satisfy needs or desires shared by most users
2. Help make progress against a business goal
3. Be possible for the team to build well and ship fast

### Prioritization Process
1. Brainstorm feature ideas (from anyone: team, sponsors, customers, competitors)
2. Organize by impact against top user needs (high/low)
3. Map high-impact ideas against business goals (high/low)
4. Focus on ideas that score high on both user impact AND business impact
5. Within that quadrant, organize by effort (build highest-impact, lowest-effort first)

### Saying No
Unless you know when to say "no," you'll end up with tangentially related features, a complex product no one is happy with, and an overworked team.

## Backlog Management

### The Backlog Is a Communication Tool
The backlog translates vision and strategy into day-to-day tactical work. The most important work is at the top. The product manager owns the backlog but gathers input from all perspectives.

### Vertical Slices, Not Horizontal
Build one small feature at a time (vertical slice through all layers) rather than building entire horizontal layers. This delivers user value frequently and enables fast feedback.

**Horizontal slices** (anti-pattern): Build all backend, then all frontend, then all UI — nothing is shippable until all layers are done.

**Vertical slices** (correct): Build one complete user-facing feature at a time — each slice delivers value independently.

### The Smaller the Better
Small stories are easier to understand, estimate, implement, and test. They enable:
- Frequent, tangible progress
- Better predictability
- Developer rotation across the codebase
- Higher bus count (less key-person risk)
- Faster value delivery

### Continuous Prioritization
Don't commit to fixed sprint scope. New information can change priorities at any time. Developers always pick up the story at the top of the backlog — so the order IS the priority.

### Backlog Structure
| Section | Purpose | Prioritized? |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Icebox** | Ideas needing more thought | No |
| **Backlog** | Ready to estimate and work on | Yes (top = highest priority) |
| **Current** | In progress | — |
| **Done** | Accepted by product manager | — |

### Epics
Groups of stories representing larger features or themes. Help convey big-picture priorities without losing the granular story view.

## User Stories

### Format
```
TITLE: Short, descriptive (e.g., "Sales Rep should be able to download proposal as PDF")

DESCRIPTION (Connextra format):
As a [TYPE OF USER]
I want [SOME GOAL]
So that [SOME BENEFIT]

ACCEPTANCE CRITERIA (Gherkin format):
GIVEN [SOME CONTEXT]
WHEN [SOME ACTION IS CARRIED OUT]
THEN [SOME CONSEQUENCES]

RESOURCES: Mocks, wireframes, user flows, data models
```

### What Makes a Good User Story
- Title is clear and descriptive
- User is clearly identified
- There's a clear beginning and end
- Acceptance criteria satisfy the user's goals
- Resources attached for non-obvious details
- Represents the smallest amount of verifiable functionality that provides incremental value

### What Makes a Bad User Story
- Vague title
- User type not identified
- No clear beginning or end
- Acceptance criteria written like multiple stories (too big)
- User's goal not identified
- No resources attached

### Event-Based System Stories
For systems without traditional user interfaces (event streaming, data processing pipelines, IoT), standard "As a user..." stories can break down. Instead:

**Focus on actions, not personified components.** Don't name components ("Evie the Event Stream"). Instead, identify the real actors and their actions.

**Identify real actors:**
- **Processors**: Consume data from incoming streams, perform calculations, emit results to outgoing streams
- **Sinks**: Consume calculation results from event streams, save/update values in data stores
- **APIs**: Query data stores to provide responses for requesting applications

**Write from the downstream consumer's perspective:**
```
As the [downstream consumer],
I want [specific data/action],
So that [value delivered].
```

**Acceptance criteria verify the component's outputs:**
```
Given [expected input] has been received,
When I read from [output stream/store],
Then I shall see [expected output].
```

**Validate incrementally** — each story should prove out distinct value and key integrations:
- Can the component receive input properly?
- Is the input data adequate for calculations?
- Are the calculations correct?
- Can the component deliver output in the right format?
- Does it perform at scale?

**Watch for technical myopia** — focusing too narrowly on functional correctness can cause you to miss non-functional requirements (throughput, processing times at scale, fault tolerance). Balance action-oriented stories with explicit non-functional stories.

## Story Types
| Type | Description | Estimated? |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| **User story** | New feature providing user/business value | Yes |
| **Chore** | Necessary but no direct user value (setup, tooling, tech debt) | No |
| **Bug** | Defect in already-accepted functionality | No |
| **Release** | Milestone marker for tracking progress | No |

### Story Points
- Relative complexity estimates (not time)
- Enable velocity tracking (average points delivered per iteration)
- Help with prioritization (one big story vs. three small ones = same effort, different risk/profile)
- Only completed stories count toward velocity

## Team Rhythm

### Daily Meetings
| Meeting | Purpose | Your Role |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| **Office standup** | Kick off day with "new faces," "helps," "interestings," "events" | Announce new teammates, ask for help, share learnings |
| **Project standup** | Check progress, plan, blockers | Understand blockers, remind of action items, rotate facilitation |

### Weekly Meetings
| Meeting | Purpose | Your Role |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| **Pre-IPM (optional)** | Ensure backlog has stories ready for estimation | Ensure stories are well-written |
| **Iteration Planning Meeting (IPM)** | Estimate complexity, clarify stories, prioritize | Communicate user/business value, confirm splitting, clarify acceptance criteria |
| **Sponsor/Stakeholder update** | Share progress, demo, raise blockers | Frame demo in context of outcomes, speak to empirical data, share assumptions tested and planned |
| **Retro** | Celebrate successes, discuss confusion, reflect on challenges | Share experiences openly, dig deeper on root causes |

### Running the Iteration Planning Meeting
1. **Frame the conversation**: Establish the iteration theme, tie to product/project goals
2. **Clarify stories**: Step through each story from top of backlog. Ensure comps, wires, assets attached. Clarify acceptance criteria.
3. **Estimate complexity**: Each developer provides point estimate. If implementation isn't clear, talk through approaches. Nominate stories for merging or splitting.
4. **Pay down debt**: Look for opportunities to wrap refactoring and tech debt into feature development
5. **Estimate two iterations max**: Keep visibility to two weeks. Don't try to plan further ahead.
6. **Keep it under one hour**: If you run out of prepared work, end the meeting rather than making up stories in real time.

## Value-Quality-Constraints Tradeoff

Product initiatives have three goals — but you can only fix two:
1. **Value**: Customer value that drives business impact
2. **Quality**: Reliability and adaptability (low design debt, low tech debt)
3. **Constraints**: People, time, scope

**Rule**: Only one constraint can be fixed. In agile organizations, it's usually time. The other two must flex.

### What Is Value?
Benefit delivered to customers through features, plus business impact created when customers engage. Measured by what customers want and the KPIs we must meet.

### What Is Quality?
- **Reliability**: Consistently good customer experience
- **Adaptability**: Ability to keep evolving the product at a predictable pace

### What Are Constraints?
Guardrails, not goals. They establish clear expectations around delivery but should not override value and quality.

## Deciding When to Ship

### Ship Early and Often
- The more we ship, the more we deliver value and validate strategy
- User testing without real software limits what we can learn
- Working software answers: Have we built enough? Are we focused on the right thing? Will we hit our KPIs?
- Keep the feedback loop as short as possible

### Release the Smallest Thing Possible
Release the smallest thing that provides data showing what users find valuable. This informs the next iteration.

### Make Releases Cheap and Repeatable
If releases are risky and cumbersome, teams push them off. A repeatable, reliable release process is critical to continuous delivery.

## Sustainable Pace

Healthy product development is a marathon, not a sprint. Maintain a sustainable pace:
- 40-hour workweek
- Predictable cadence
- Continuous delivery without burnout
- Team members stay motivated and effective long-term

## Communication

### The Product Manager as Communicator
- Frame work in the context of outcomes, not features
- Speak to empirical data guiding decisions
- Share assumptions tested and planned
- Communicate blockers clearly: what you need, why, and by when
- Keep stakeholders, sponsors, and team aligned

### Roadmap as Communication Tool
The roadmap is your most important communication tool. It creates visibility for people outside the immediate team. Use it to:
- Help stakeholders understand vision, goals, and next steps
- Coordinate joint efforts and dependencies with other teams
- Surface important conversations early

## Self-Correction
- Roadmap too detailed/fixed? Reframe around outcomes, not features
- Backlog too large? Prune aggressively — if it's not in the current/near-term horizon, icebox it
- Stories too big? Split into smaller vertical slices
- Team overworked? Protect sustainable pace, reduce scope (not quality)
- Vanity metrics on dashboards? Replace with actionable ratios
- Assumptions untested? Design lean experiments before building
- Stakeholders misaligned? Use roadmap as the shared visual
